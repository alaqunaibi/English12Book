⭐ ACTIVE VOCABULARY | Collocations

Collocations are words that naturally go together. A common collocation is verb + noun, e.g., tilt your head.

• Strong collocations: the connection is fixed — e.g., She pursed her lips. He clapped his hands.

• Weak collocations: a word collocates with many others — e.g., tilt collocates with head, chin, but also boat, chair, plane.

💡 المتلازمات اللفظية (Collocations) المتلازمات اللفظية هي كلمات تُستخدم معاً بشكل طبيعي في اللغة الإنجليزية.
مثال: tilt your head = تميل رأسك (لا يمكن استبدال tilt بـ bend)

متلازمة قوية: الكلمة ترتبط بكلمة واحدة فقط — مثل: purse your lips (تضمّ شفتيك)
متلازمة ضعيفة: الكلمة تُستخدم مع كلمات كثيرة — مثل: tilt مع head أو boat أو chair

مقالة القراءة: ارتدِ وجهاً شجاعاً! — أم هل يجب ذلك؟
Original English Article

Put on a Brave Face!
Or… should we?

1 While we are all well aware that moving home, starting a new job or receiving bad news, for example, can considerably raise anxiety levels, it's not always as easy to make the connection between other physical health problems that we may experience as a result of stress. It would seem logical to ascribe a racing heart and raised blood pressure to nervousness and apprehension, but would we automatically link those nerves to an upset stomach or back pain? The truth is that our emotions can trigger a host of physical reactions and that is why we need to concern ourselves with maintaining our emotional health.

2 "That's easier said than done", you might say, and you would be right. Emotions are not easy to control, particularly negative ones. So, is it actually possible to make ourselves feel less negative? 'How to become a happier you' is a hot topic at the moment and we are inundated with advice and tips online. There is an abundance of websites with surveys, questionnaires, tips and shared personal experiences available to us. Even schools are running mindfulness classes for their students as part of the curriculum.

3 A new take on the topic, based on the link between emotion and facial expression, is that rather than frown when we're feeling low or upset, we should try smiling. Studies have shown that facial expressions not only reflect emotions, but influence them. In other words, we may smile because we are happy, but we can also feel happy because we smile!

4 So, do other facial expressions affect our mood too? Apparently, they do. There are studies that show that if we are experiencing pain, yet endeavour not to show it on our faces, the degree of pain we experience can be less intense than if we wince and frown. Likewise, if we show that we are frightened when faced with danger, this can actually exacerbate the fear that we're feeling. According to the experts, this is because the way we experience emotion does not just involve the brain; it is far more complex. However, not all scientists are in complete agreement over the benefit of suppressing emotions or faking facial expressions to influence our moods. Research suggests that although faking emotions can help in the short term, continually suppressing emotions can have a negative effect on our general health. If the face is not involved in expressing an emotion, that emotion may eventually find another outlet, such as depression, a pessimistic outlook, or even physical illness.

5 Linked with this latter belief is the concept of emotional labour, which was first identified in 1983. A recent interpretation of the term, started by an American journalist in 2017, references the need for people to concern themselves with routine chores that often result in dissatisfaction. However, the term was originally invented to describe the requirement for certain employees, for instance those in the service industries such as flight attendants and shop assistants, to present a happy face to clients, however bad they may be feeling. In other words, it is part of their job to pretend to be happy and hide any negative emotions. This faking of emotion can contribute to emotional exhaustion, leaving the person drained with little emotional energy left.

6 Whilst wallowing in negativity is not going to help anyone, there are definitely times when we should allow ourselves to show our true feelings. Balancing when or when not to express emotions is something we need to be aware of for the sake of our health.

الفقرة 1: بينما ندرك جميعاً أن الانتقال إلى منزل جديد أو البدء في وظيفة جديدة أو تلقّي أخبار سيئة قد يرفع مستويات القلق بشكل كبير، فإنه ليس من السهل دائماً ربط مشاكل الصحة الجسدية الأخرى بالتوتر. يبدو منطقياً نسب سرعة نبضات القلب وارتفاع ضغط الدم إلى التوتر والقلق، لكن هل نربط تلقائياً تلك التوترات بآلام المعدة أو أوجاع الظهر؟ الحقيقة هي أن مشاعرنا يمكنها إطلاق سلسلة من ردود الفعل الجسدية، ولهذا يجب أن نهتم بالحفاظ على صحتنا العاطفية.

الفقرة 2: قد تقول "هذا أسهل مما يُقال"، وستكون على حق. المشاعر ليست سهلة التحكم، لا سيما السلبية منها. فهل من الممكن فعلاً أن نجعل أنفسنا نشعر بقدر أقل من السلبية؟ موضوع "كيف تصبح أكثر سعادة" رائج جداً الآن، ونحن مثقلون بالنصائح والتلميحات عبر الإنترنت.

الفقرة 3: نظرة جديدة على الموضوع تقوم على الرابط بين المشاعر وتعابير الوجه؛ بدلاً من التجهّم عندما نشعر بالحزن، يجب أن نحاول الابتسام. أظهرت الدراسات أن تعابير الوجه لا تعكس المشاعر فحسب، بل تؤثر فيها أيضاً!

الفقرة 4: إذا كنا نعاني من الألم وحاولنا إخفاءه، فقد يكون الألم أقل حدة مما لو أظهرناه. وبالمثل، إظهار الخوف يمكن أن يُفاقم الخوف ذاته. ليس جميع العلماء متفقين على فائدة كبت المشاعر؛ فرغم أن تزييف المشاعر قد يساعد على المدى القصير، فإن كبتها باستمرار قد يؤثر سلباً على الصحة العامة.

الفقرة 5: مرتبط بهذا مفهوم العمل العاطفي الذي تم التعرف عليه أول مرة عام 1983. كان المصطلح في الأصل يصف متطلّبات بعض الموظفين — كمضيفي الطيران وعمال المتاجر — بتقديم وجه سعيد للعملاء مهما كانوا يشعرون. هذا التظاهر بالمشاعر يمكن أن يُسهم في الإرهاق العاطفي.

الفقرة 6: بينما لن يفيد التمرّغ في السلبية أحداً، هناك أوقات يجب فيها السماح لأنفسنا بإظهار مشاعرنا الحقيقية. الموازنة بين التعبير عن المشاعر وإخفائها أمرٌ يجب أن ندركه من أجل صحتنا.

⚠️ WATCH OUT!

Some verbs follow more than one pattern, sometimes with a change in register:

• He suggested watching a film. (less formal – gerund)

• He suggested (that) we (should) watch a film. (more formal – that clause)

Full Article Text
A

People! Traffic! Texts! Work! Modern life can be so stressful, can't it? Given the relentless pace of life and the ever-present screens that we all rely on for our jobs, studying and entertainment, it can be very difficult to really escape from it all. The situation begs the question: were humans really meant to live with this amount of constant stimulation? It's difficult to reasonably conclude that the answer is 'yes'.

B

One increasingly popular response to this constant blur of activity is forest bathing, or as it is known in Japan where it originated: shinrin-yoku. The term was coined by Tomohide Akiyama, Director of the Ministry of Agriculture, Forestry and Fisheries for Japan. He found that compounds and essential oils which trees naturally emit had very positive effects on human health. With this in mind, Akiyama got the practice officially recognised, increasing the value of forests within the nation's psyche. This happened during the 1980s when much of the world was on the edge of the technological revolution.

C

So what exactly do forest bathers do? Firstly, leave your phone and gadgets behind. The aim is to completely immerse yourself in natural surroundings. A forest is ideal, but spending time in a park or any place with an abundance of nature can also yield great results. Set aside plenty of time — ideally four to six hours. This is so you can contemplate, relax and slow your heart rate. Focus on engaging your senses: listen to birdsong, inhale the fresh air, watch the creatures you see and touch the plant life along your way.

D

Following forest bathing, practitioners report improved mood and mental health, decreased blood pressure and stress, and an increased ability to fight sickness and disease. So, why not try spending an afternoon quietly walking through a forest? Alternatively, perhaps simply slip off your shoes and walk barefoot on the grass for a while? Either way, your body and mind will thank you for taking a break from screens and reconnecting with nature.

Story: Against the Odds

Against the Odds

Maha Al-Bargouthi was born in 1962 in Amman. She developed paraplegia due to polio at birth, which affected her legs. Rather than choosing a quiet life, she embraced sport from age 11 — athletics and table tennis. She faced discrimination and inadequate sports facilities, yet saw these obstacles as "rungs on the ladder to her success."

Her parents credited with being her biggest fans treated her no differently from her classmates, fuelling her confidence and self-reliance. At the Berlin Special Olympics in 1995, her 200-metre wheelchair run set a world record at 32.26 seconds. Just five years later at Sydney's Paralympics, Maha won Jordan's first ever gold medal in para table tennis singles.

She went on to win eight medals altogether at the Paralympics, World Championships, Pan Arab Games and Asian Championships. Her advice: "Seek out and try to achieve your dream, no matter how difficult it may be. The keys to success are perseverance, determination, and faith in one's own abilities."

قصة مها البرغوثي

ضد كل التوقعات

وُلدت مها البرغوثي عام 1962 في عمّان. أصيبت بشلل الأطفال عند الولادة مما أدى إلى إصابتها بالشلل النصفي. بدلاً من الاستسلام، انخرطت في الرياضة منذ سن الحادية عشرة. واجهت التمييز ومنشآت رياضية لا تلائم احتياجاتها، لكنها رأت هذه العقبات كـ"درجات في سلّم نجاحها".

كان لوالديها الفضل الأكبر في تنشئتها بثقة ودون التمييز بينها وبين زملائها. في برلين 1995، حطّمت الرقم القياسي العالمي لسباق 200م على الكرسي المتحرك في 32.26 ثانية. وبعد خمس سنوات فقط في سيدني، فازت بأول ميدالية ذهبية أردنية في البارالمبياد.

نصيحتها الذهبية: "اسعَ لتحقيق حلمك مهما كان صعباً. الإعاقة الحقيقية هي تعطيل رغبتك في تحقيق أهدافك. مفاتيح النجاح هي: المثابرة، والإصرار، والإيمان بقدراتك."
